Dancing in the Moonlight

Part IB Labs - I1\ Spectrum Analysis and Amplitude Modulation

Published on January 1, 1

Summary

This experiment aims to:

  • Introduce the concept of spectral analysis and demonstrate how signals can be decomposed into their frequency components

  • Measure and analyze the spectra of basic waveforms including sine, square, and triangular waves

  • Investigate the frequency spectrum of amplitude-modulated (AM) signals and explore the effects of varying modulation parameters

  • Examine the operation of basic demodulator circuits for recovering the original information signal

Theory

Spectrum Analysis

A periodic signal with period \(T\) can be expressed using the Fourier series: \[x(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}{a_n \cos(n\omega_0 t) + b_n \sin(n\omega_0 t)}\] where \(\omega_0 = \frac{2\pi}{T}\) is the fundamental frequency. The mean-squared value of each term \(a_n \cos(n\omega_0 t) + b_n \sin(n\omega_0 t)\) is proportional to \(\sqrt{a_n^2 + b_n^2}\), allowing the spectrum to be represented as a series of \(\delta\)-functions centered at frequencies \(n\omega_0\) and weighted by the corresponding terms \(\sqrt{a_n^2 + b_n^2}\).

Amplitude Modulation

In amplitude modulation (AM), an information signal modulates the amplitude of a higher-frequency carrier signal. For an information signal \(x(t) = E\cos(\omega t)\) and a carrier signal \(x_c(t) = E_c\cos(\omega_c t)\), the amplitude modulated signal is:

\[x_m(t) = E_c(1 + m\cos(\omega t))\cos(\omega_c t)\]

where \(m = \frac{E}{E_c}\) is the modulation index. This can be expanded to:

\[x_m(t) = E_c\cos(\omega_c t) + \frac{mE_c}{2}\cos((\omega_c + \omega_0)t) + \frac{mE_c}{2}\cos((\omega_c - \omega_0)t)\]

This expression shows that the modulated signal contains three frequency components: the carrier frequency \(\omega_c\) and two sidebands at \(\omega_c + \omega_0\) and \(\omega_c - \omega_0\).

In the frequency domain, the AM signal’s spectrum shows:

\[ \begin{gathered} X_m(\omega) = {E_c\pi}[\delta(\omega - \omega_c) + \delta(\omega + \omega_c)] + \\ \frac{mE_c\pi}{2}[\delta(\omega - (\omega_c + \omega_0)) + \delta(\omega + (\omega_c + \omega_0))] + \\ \frac{mE_c\pi}{2}[\delta(\omega - (\omega_c - \omega_0)) + \delta(\omega + (\omega_c - \omega_0))] \end{gathered} \]

Demodulation

The diode-capacitor demodulation circuit shown in the handout works on the principle of envelope detection. When the input AM signal exceeds the capacitor voltage, the diode conducts and the capacitor charges to track the signal. When the signal falls below the capacitor voltage, the diode stops conducting and the capacitor discharges through the resistor with time constant \(RC\). When we connect a time varying input as \(x_m(t)\), the capacitor will alternate between charging and discharging cycles. During the charging cycle, the capacitor’s voltage \(v(t)\) will track the input or \(v(t) = x_m(t)\). During the discharging cycle, \(v(t)\) will decay. Discharge will persist until \(x_m(t)\) rises to match the falling value of \(v(t)\), when the circuit switches to charging. Charging will end when \(x_m(t)\) begins to fall again.

To summarise, if the last switching time (when \(x_m(t)\) and \(v(t)\) crossed) was \(\tau\), then until the next crossover, the output voltage is given by

\[ v(t) = \begin{cases} v(\tau)e^{-\frac{1}{RC}(t-\tau)} & \text{if } x_m(t) < v(\tau)e^{-\frac{1}{RC}(t-\tau)} \\ x_m(t) & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Results and Discussion

Spectra of Simple Periodic Signals

Sine Wave Analysis

For a 2 kHz sine wave, the spectrum showed a single frequency component exactly at 2 kHz, as expected from the theory.

The amplitude of this component directly corresponds to the amplitude of the sine wave in the time domain. The spectrum analyzer proves more precise for frequency measurements compared to estimates from oscilloscope time divisions.

Square Wave Analysis

The Fourier series expansion of a perfect square wave with peak-to-peak amplitude 2 and period \(T\) is: \[z(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \sin(n\omega_0 t)\] where \(\omega_0 = \frac{2\pi}{T}\) and \[b_n = \begin{cases} \frac{4}{\pi}\frac{1}{n}, & \text{if } n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

For the experimental square wave with frequency 1 kHz and peak-to-peak amplitude 2.0 V, the measured spectrum showed harmonics at odd multiples of the fundamental frequency (1 kHz, 3 kHz, 5 kHz, etc.), with amplitudes decreasing proportionally to \(\frac{1}{n}\) as predicted by theory.

When removing the anti-aliasing filter, additional high-frequency components appeared in the spectrum due to aliasing effects, demonstrating the importance of proper filtering before digital sampling.

Triangular Wave Analysis

For a triangular wave, the theoretical Fourier coefficients are:

\[ b_n = \begin{cases} \frac{8}{\pi^2}(-1)^{(n-1)/2}\frac{1}{n^2}, & \text{if } n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Since a triangular wave can be formed by convolving a square wave with a square pulse, the Fourier coefficients are expected to decrease as \(\frac{1}{n^2}\) rather than \(\frac{1}{n}\) for the square wave. This is because convolution in the time domain corresponds to multiplication in the frequency domain, adding another factor of \(\frac{1}{n}\) to the decay rate.

Amplitude Modulation

Theoretical Spectrum

The amplitude modulated signal \(x_m(t) = E_c(1 + m\cos(\omega t))\cos(\omega_c t)\) has a frequency domain representation with components at:

  • The carrier frequency \(\omega_c\) with amplitude \(E_c\)

  • Two sidebands at \(\omega_c \pm \omega\) with amplitudes \(\frac{mE_c}{2}\) each

This spectrum allows multiple signals to be transmitted simultaneously over a common medium, with each signal modulating a different carrier frequency, enabling frequency-division multiplexing.

Modulation Index Analysis

The carrier sine wave was set to 8 kHz and modulated with a 1 kHz signal. The resulting modulated waveform showed the characteristic amplitude envelope as seen in Figure 6.

From the oscilloscope display, the maximum and minimum amplitudes of the envelope were measured, allowing the calculation of the modulation index: \[\frac{1+m}{1-m} = \frac{E_{max}}{E_{min}}\]

For the observed waveform with \(E_{max} = 3\) V and \(E_{min} = 0.05\) V: \[\frac{1+m}{1-m} = 60\] Solving for \(m\) gives \(m = 0.96\).

Effect of \(m = 1\)

When the modulation index was increased to \(m = 1\), the minimum amplitude of the modulated signal approached zero, as expected because \(E_{min} = E_c(1-m)\). With \(m = 1\), this gives \(E_{min} = 0\).

The spectrum should show a reduced carrier component relative to the sidebands, with the sideband amplitudes increasing proportionally.

Suppressed Carrier Modulation

By adjusting the carrier level to its minimum, we achieved suppressed carrier modulation where the component at the carrier frequency (middle peak) was eliminated from the spectrum. The waveform distinctly differs from standard AM, showing phase reversals where the modulation envelope crosses zero. In this case, \(m \rightarrow \infty\) as \(E_c \rightarrow 0\), resulting in a spectrum with only the sideband components.

Amplitude Demodulation

RC Time Constant Selection

For effective demodulation, the RC time constant should satisfy: \[\frac{1}{\omega_c} \ll RC \ll \frac{1}{\omega_0}\]

For our system with carrier frequency \(f_c = 8\) kHz and modulation frequency \(f = 1\) kHz: \[\frac{1}{2\pi \times 8000} \ll RC \ll \frac{1}{2\pi \times 1000}\] \[20 \mu{s} \ll RC \ll 160 \mu{s}\]

A suitable value is \(RC = 100\mu{s}\), which can be achieved with \(R = 100k\Omega\) and \(C = 1\) nF.

If \(RC\) is too small, the capacitor will discharge too quickly and follow the carrier frequency rather than just the envelope. If \(RC\) is too large, the capacitor won’t discharge quickly enough to follow the modulation frequency accurately.

Demodulation Circuit Analysis

The demodulator circuit was connected to the modulated signal output, and waveforms were observed at the input, output, and intermediate test points (TP1 and TP2).

Test point TP1 only allows positive half-cycles to pass through. The output showed the demodulated signal following the envelope of the modulated waveform, with some ripple at the carrier frequency. The spectrum of the demodulated signal showed a strong component at the modulation frequency (1 kHz) and some residual components at the carrier frequency (8 kHz) due to the rectification process.

Low-Pass Filter Design

To improve the demodulator performance for suppressed carrier modulation, a low-pass filter was designed with a 3dB cutoff frequency at 5 kHz, midway between the information frequency (1 kHz) and the carrier frequency (8 kHz).

For a simple RC low-pass filter: \[|V(\omega)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + \omega^2 R^2 C^2}} |U(\omega)|\]

At the 3dB point where gain = \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\): \[\omega^2 R^2 C^2 = 1\] \[(2\pi \times 5000)^2 (RC)^2 = 1\] \[RC = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 5000} \approx 32 \mu{s}\]

Suitable component values are \(R = 10k\Omega\) and \(C = 3.2\) nF. This filter effectively attenuated the carrier frequency components while passing the information signal at 1 kHz.

Conclusions

This experiment demonstrated the principles of spectral analysis and amplitude modulation through practical measurements. Key findings include:

  • The spectrum analyzer effectively revealed frequency components of basic waveforms, with measured harmonic amplitudes closely matching theoretical predictions based on Fourier analysis.

  • Amplitude modulation creates a signal with three key spectral components: the carrier frequency and two sidebands at frequencies offset by the modulation frequency.

  • The modulation index \(m\) significantly affects the modulated signal characteristics:

    • With \(m < 1\), the carrier retains the largest amplitude in the spectrum

    • With suppressed carrier modulation (\(m \to \infty\)), the carrier component disappears, conserving transmission power

  • Demodulation can be achieved with a simple diode-capacitor circuit, with the RC time constant being critical for optimal performance. Additional filtering can improve the recovered signal quality, particularly for suppressed carrier modulation
