Dancing in the Moonlight

Part IB Labs E4\ Wave Transmission and Reflection

Published on January 1, 1

Summary

This experiment investigates the propagation and reflection characteristics of microwaves. The primary aims are:

  • Measure the wavelength and phase velocity in air by analyzing standing waves in a coaxial line.

  • Use uncertainty analysis to quantitatively assess the accuracy of the results.

  • Characterize the refractive index \(n\) of a material by measuring the change in travel time when the material is substituted for air.

  • Construct a microwave resonator using a section line with highly reflective ends.

Introduction

Electromagnetic waves are defined by their frequency (\(f\)), wavelength (\(\lambda\)), and phase velocity (\(u\)), with the relation \[u = f\lambda. \label{eq:phase_velocity}\] In free space, this velocity is approximately \(c\approx3.0\times10^8\,\mathrm{m/s}\). However, guided propagation (in a coaxial or strip line) allows for the observation of standing waves due to the interference between a forward and reflected wave. The positions of minima (nodes) and maxima occur at intervals of \(\lambda/2\), enabling an accurate determination of \(\lambda\). When dielectric materials are introduced into the waveguide, the propagation speed decreases according to \[u_{\text{material}} = \frac{c}{n}, \label{eq:material_velocity}\] where \(n\) is the refractive index.

In addition, the reflection of waves at boundaries (with either a closed or open end) can be quantified via the power reflection coefficient,

\[ \rho = \frac{B}{A}, \label{eq:reflection_coeff}\] with \(A\) and \(B\) representing the amplitudes of the forward and reflected waves, respectively.

Experimental Apparatus and Methodology

The setup comprises:

  • Microwave Source: A microprocessor-controlled generator (2–4 GHz) providing a continuous-wave signal.

  • Slotted Line: A coaxial line with an adjustable probe that scans along the line to detect minima in the standing wave pattern.

  • Strip Line: A configuration permitting the insertion of dielectric materials and the formation of resonant cavities using brass screws.

  • Data Logger: Integrated with the signal source for real-time measurement of the signal amplitude.

The experiment was divided into four main parts:

  1. Wavelength and Phase Velocity: Measurements of minima positions were taken for three distinct frequencies to calculate \(\lambda\) and hence \(u\).

  2. Reflection Coefficients: By switching between a short-circuit plug and an open-circuit plug, shifts in the minima were recorded and used to determine the reflection coefficient.

  3. Dielectric Material Analysis: Dielectric sheets (teflon, perspex, and glass) were inserted, and the corresponding shifts in the standing wave pattern were used to compute the refractive index via the change in effective line length.

  4. Resonator Response: A resonator was constructed by placing two brass screws in the strip line. Frequency sweeps were performed for two different screw spacings to analyze the resonant transmission characteristics.

Uncertainty Analysis

The wavelength \(\lambda\) is determined from the distance between successive minima. If \(P\) and \(Q\) are the measured positions of two minima (with uncertainty \(\Delta P\) and \(\Delta Q\)), then: \[\lambda = 2\frac{(Q-P)}{N},\] where \(N\) is the number of half-wavelength intervals between the measurements. The relative uncertainty in \(\lambda\) is: \[\frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda} = \frac{\Delta P + \Delta Q}{Q-P}.\] Since the phase velocity is given by \(u = f\lambda\), its relative uncertainty is: \[\frac{\Delta u}{u} = \frac{\Delta f}{f} + \frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda}.\]

We estimate the absolute uncertainties of the measurements as the smallest division. \(\Delta f = \pm 0.001 \text{ GHz}\) and \(\Delta P = \Delta Q = \pm 1 \text{ mm}\). The calculated uncertainties are shown in Table 2 . The accuracy of values is quite high, with only around 0.5% uncertainty.

Uncertainty Analysis

The uncertainty of \(n\) is calculated as follows:

\[n = \frac{\frac{(P_2 - P_1) - (Q_2 - Q_1))}{2}}{\frac{c}{f}}\]

The relative uncertainty in \(n\) is given by:

\[\frac{\Delta n}{n} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{2 \Delta P}{P_2 - P_1} + \frac{2 \Delta Q}{Q_2 - Q_1} \right) + \frac{\Delta f}{f}\]

Substituting in the values, we get a relative uncertainty of about 7%. The standing wave pattern typically shifts by \(\lambda/4\), hence the theoretical value of \(n\) is \(0.25\). However, due to the high experimental uncertainty, we have our measured values deviating significantly to the theoretical value.

The standing wave ratio \(S\) is computed as: \[S^2 = \frac{\text{A+B} - \text{Offset}}{\text{A-B} - \text{Offset}}\] and the reflection coefficient is then given by: \[\rho = \frac{S-1}{S+1}\] This gives us \(S=7.46\) and \(\rho = 0.76\). This value indicates that the open-circuit plug is nearly as reflective as the short-circuit plug, though not ideal.

Propagation in Dielectric Materials

Dielectric samples (teflon, perspex, and glass) of various thicknesses were inserted beneath the strip line and ground plane. The insertion causes a shift in the minima. The refractive index is determined by: \[n = 1 + \frac{R - M}{d},\] where \(M\) is the original minima position, \(R\) is the shifted position when the sample is inserted, and \(d\) is the sample thickness. Uncertainty Analysis

The relative uncertainty in \(n\) is: \[\frac{\Delta n}{n} = \frac{\Delta R + \Delta M}{R-M} + \frac{\Delta d}{d}.\] Assuming \(\Delta R = \Delta M = \Delta d = \pm1\) mm, the percentage uncertainty in \(n\) will vary with the sample thickness, typically ranging from about 5% for thicker samples to nearly 30% for thinner ones. The relative uncertainty decreases with increasing sample thickness.

Resonator Transmission Characteristics

A microwave resonator was formed by lightly screwing two brass pins into the strip line. For a spacing of 120 mm, a single resonance was observed near 2338 MHz, yielding a wavelength of approximately 128 mm, slightly larger than the physical spacing. The effective resonator length, expressed in half-wavelength multiples, is slightly larger than the physical spacing. This discrepancy is possibly due to fringing electric fields and the finite size of the reflectors.

Discussion

The measured phase velocities in air were consistently close to the theoretical value of \(300\,\mathrm{mm/ns}\), confirming the validity of the standing-wave method. The observed shift in minima when changing from a short-circuit to an open-circuit termination reflects the phase difference introduced at the boundary, and the calculated reflection coefficient (\(\rho\approx0.76\)) indicates incomplete reflection with the open plug.

The refractive index results show a trend where the effective \(n\) decreases with increasing sample thickness, though variations among samples suggest that experimental uncertainties—such as positioning errors and material nonuniformities—play a significant role. Finally, the resonator experiments clearly demonstrate that the effective cavity length can exceed the physical spacing due to factors like fringing fields.

Conclusions

In summary:

  • The wavelength and phase velocity of microwaves in air were accurately determined using standing-wave measurements.

  • Different terminations (short vs. open ends) produced measurable phase shifts

  • The reflection coefficient of a brass pin can be calculated from the maxima and minima amplitudes, where we saw the brass pin was not a perfect reflector

  • Inserting dielectric materials under the strip line enabled the estimation of various refractive indices, although uncertainties were significant for thinner samples.

  • Resonator measurements indicated that the effective length is greater than the physical spacing, likely due to fringing and imperfect terminations.

These results are in good agreement with theoretical expectations and demonstrate the key principles of wave transmission and reflection in guided systems. $$
